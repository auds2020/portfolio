About this

The objective of this project was to generally get a lot more comfortable in the metal shop and use as many tools as possible, including the hand shear, slip roll, tube bender, ring roller, ironworker shear, hand plasma cutter, ironworker punch, cold saw, and portaband saw.

For my design, I wanted to create a little DJ who would nod along to music and scratch records. In order to do this, it'd require three different cams on a crank shaft: one for each record and one for the DJ's head. I began by CADing my idea so I could get a sense of scale and a better idea of what materials I'd need to use. I took inspiration from automotive parts and online welded sculptures, and decided to make the main body out of nuts and bolts.

Once I had a better idea of how it would come together, I began by welding the main body.

Hardware

We decided to make a back mounting surface out of 3'x5' plywood. After applying several coats of wipe-on polyeurathane finish, we mounted some off-the-shelf hooks for our helmets, skateboard mounts, and the pegboard. We used two collapsible shelf brackets and another piece of plywood to create a collapsible work surface. Finally, we mounted the whole thing to the side of our garage by first drilling out the concrete with a masonry bit, then using conrete anchors to secure it to the wall.

Electronics

The electronics are driven by an Arduino Nano, which takes in I2C communcation from two color sensors mounted on each skateboard's charging brick. If the LED indicator on the charging brick is red, that indicates that the board is charging but isn't fully charged. If it's green, it's fully charged or not plugged in. The Arduino then sends the corresponding RGB signal to the corresponding side of addressable RGB LED strip, which has its own power supply to minimize current draw from the Arduino. This entire assembly is consolidated onto a single protoboard.
