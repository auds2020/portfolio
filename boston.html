Boston Light-up Skyline

  • CategoryFor fun
  • Project dateJanuary 2026 (weekend hackathon)
  • Skills usedAdobe Illustrator, laser cutting, CAD

Motivation

I've lived in Boston since 2021, and I think it's one of the coziest cities I've ever been to! In celebration of Boston, our hackathon team (2 MIT grad students and me) wanted to make a 2'x2' light up map of Boston.

Electronics

This project uses an industrial LED panel - the kind that you typically see in the ceiling tiles of offices. I thought 2'x2' would be a good size for wall art without being too unwieldy. The LED panel we selected has sliders on the backside controlling color temperature and brightness, but we wanted to make the brightness control more easily accessible, so we added a 10V dimmer switch.

The Frame

First, I CAD'ed the entire assembly, making sure you could still access the electronics panel with the frame on. I made a frame out of cherry using the table saw, mitre saw, and router. To provide a stable, flat surface for all of the later layers to rest on top of, I cut a rectangle out of frosted acrylic and cut a different slot for it in the frame so that it would be held in place.

The Layers

I used Cadmapper to generate an Adobe Illustrator file of the roads and bodies of water in Boston. After cleaning up the file so it was manufacturble, we laser cut a sheet of plywood to represent the roads and blue acrylic to represent the water. We used superglue to glue the layers onto one another, adding shims under the plywood so it didn't flex.

The Buildings

This was part of the Formlabs hackathon, so it was only natural we resin printed something! We selected a couple key landmarks of personal importance and some famous landmarks, which we then CAD'ed. We decided to print the whole "city block" as a shim to the frosted acrylic, which would then puzzle piece nicely into the roads. The resolution of the SLA printers was fantastic, and we were able to print in clear so the light would still shine through.

Challenges

Because this was for a hackathon, we had to coordinate diligently to get all 4 frames manufactured in such a short amount of time. The plywood laser cut was very detailed, so laser cutting that alone took around 90 minutes per sheet. Addiitonally, because of a winter storm, our plywood got delayed and woudln't make it in time, so we had to go buy them which was tricky to find since it's so thin/large.

What we learned

I learned that gluing a large transparent acrylic sheet to something is very hard, because you inevitably see the lines where the glue are vs just air. We transistioned from thick CA glue to thin CA glue with the consistency of water, which helped but didn't fully resolve the challenge. If I were to do it again, I would've hidden "glue zones" which would be covered by the plywood layer.
